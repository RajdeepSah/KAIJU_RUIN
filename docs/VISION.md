# REALM OF GORYO: SHADOW OF GIANTS
## Product Vision Document

**Working title:** Realm of Goryo: Shadow of Giants (placeholder, to be validated with the rights holder)
**Document:** Vision.md, v0.1 draft
**Status:** Pre-production. This document precedes the design brief. It defines why the game exists, what it must feel like, and where it goes over three-plus years. The design brief (produced after the studio intake interview) will define exactly what gets built first.

---

## 1. One-Line Pitch

A mature, comic-book-styled 2D fighting game for Android, set in the Realm of Goryo novels: human, caudata, and stranger champions battle in the shadow of god-sized kaiju, in a world where the monsters are not the opponents, they are the weather.

---

## 2. Why This Game, Why Now

Mortal Kombat Mobile and Injustice Mobile proved that a fighting game built natively for touch, with tap and swipe combat, short sessions, and a collectible roster, can hold tens of millions of players for a decade. Shadow Fight proved that a smaller studio can compete in the same space with strong art identity instead of a console license. What no one has shipped is a serious, mature, kaiju-saga fighting game on mobile. The kaiju audience is large, underserved on phones, and starved for anything that treats giant monsters as mythology rather than as a skin pack.

Realm of Goryo is unusually well shaped for this. It is a multi-book geopolitical kaiju saga with named champions across at least four rival factions, non-human playable species (caudatas, a werefox, an alien), god-tier monsters that should never be reduced to ordinary opponents, and a built-in interactive-fiction experiment (Khulandra Attacks!: Make Your Escape) that hands the game its story-mode DNA for free. The game and the novels can feed each other: every season of the game is a gateway into a book, and every new book is a content drop for the game.

## 3. Source Material Foundation (Research Digest)

This section is the canon spine the game must respect. It is synthesized from the official site copy for all seven published works plus genre context. Items marked *(inference)* are educated reads that must be verified against the books and the official press kit before they harden into design.

### 3.1 The saga, in reading order

**The Four Pillars.** By 2061, Japan has been overrun by kaiju. Threats loom "from the bottom of the ocean to distant planets." The world unites to reclaim the planet while scientists and soldiers in Japan work carefully to avoid triggering a world war. This is the founding image of the whole IP: humanity operating at human scale inside a monster-scale crisis.

**The Culling.** The world has barely settled "since the rise of Mu" when a new threat approaches. To prepare the world for the coming danger, a figure named Tengi must first cull it. A protector whose method is mass harm: this is the series' moral signature, and it is fighting-game gold.

**Baptism.** Cities are destroyed, recovery is failing, and "millions have been left voiceless in a world that is leaving them behind." Kaiju once contained in Japan spread across the globe. New dangers rise in Mu and feed an insurrection. The saga's theme of the abandoned and the unheard starts here.

**The Riven Mother.** The world braces for the coming of Raisha, the Riven Mother. Arikura urges the UN and Mu to stand down. Noah Sato is on the run with the werefox Kest toward the kaiju graveyard, where Kest intends to unleash Raisha and usher in a new era for the kaiju. Human and caudata prepare together, which confirms caudatas as a coequal people, not background creatures.

**Arms Race.** Drums of war. Shadow actors maneuver for victory. A new fear-spreading threat rises in Mu. In Anquisheng, the caudatas' dream of freedom becomes a nightmare as kaiju descend on their home. Noah Sato hides "from both man and monster."

**Monster in the Machine.** "The king is dead, and the world is about to change." Hidden in Tokyo, Noah Sato builds the Ark, "a world of forever," the next stage of civilization, a dream born from a world that abandoned him and nurtured by the alien terrorist Satori, whose own plan is to destroy the old world and build a new one "on the backs of the kaiju." Which king died is not stated in the marketing copy and must be confirmed *(open canon question)*.

**Khulandra Attacks!: Make Your Escape.** An interactive choose-your-path book. The kaiju Khulandra rises from the depths and attacks the city; the reader survives, or does not, through "dozens of horrific endings." The tagline "Goryo won't save you this time" establishes Goryo as the realm's guardian kaiju *(strongly implied)* and establishes that this IP already embraces brutal, darkly playful death scenes in an interactive format. That matters enormously for a Mature-rated adaptation.

### 3.2 The world map of forces

Four to five factions emerge cleanly, which is exactly the structure a fighting-game roster and story mode want. The World Coalition: the UN, the united governments, and the Japan-based scientists and soldiers of The Four Pillars. Mu: a risen power, source of insurrection and fear-wielding new threats; the name almost certainly nods to the lost Pacific continent of classic pulp and kaiju cinema *(inference)*. Anquisheng and the caudatas: an oppressed amphibian people; "Caudata" is the taxonomic order of salamanders, so these are very likely salamander-folk *(inference)*, which is a visually distinctive, ownable species for a fighter. The Ark circle: Noah Sato's transhuman project and Satori's apocalyptic patronage, technology as both refuge and threat. The kaiju ascendancy: Kest and anyone else who wants the monsters to inherit the Earth.

Above all factions stand the kaiju themselves: Goryo the guardian, Khulandra the destroyer from the depths, Raisha the Riven Mother, and whatever sleeps in the kaiju graveyard. The books consistently frame them as forces of nature and objects of belief, not as characters you simply defeat.

### 3.3 Naming and folklore layer

The proper nouns are soaked in Japanese folklore: a goryō is a vengeful or venerated spirit, a satori is a mind-reading yōkai, a werefox is a kitsune by another name, and Tengi echoes tengu *(observed pattern, treat as inference)*. Combined with Noah Sato building an Ark for a "world of forever," the series deliberately fuses flood myth, yōkai lore, and near-future technothriller. The game's art, music, and monster design should honor that fusion rather than defaulting to generic sci-fi.

### 3.4 Tone calibration

Reader reviews praise the suspense, the saga momentum, and, notably, the "clean language" of the mainline novels. The gamebook, meanwhile, trades in dozens of horrific endings. The honest read: this IP is dark and lethal in event, restrained in depiction. A Mortal Kombat-style M rating is therefore a deliberate escalation, not a neutral translation, and it should be made in partnership with the rights holder. Section 6 (Pillar 4) defines how we earn the M rating in a way that stays true to the books.

### 3.5 Confidence and gaps

Confirmed from official copy: the seven works, the 2061 timeframe, the named characters (Noah Sato, Satori, Kest, Tengi, Arikura), the named kaiju (Goryo, Khulandra, Raisha), the places and peoples (Mu, Anquisheng, caudatas, the kaiju graveyard, the Ark), and the major plot beats summarized above. Not yet known: canonical appearances, fighting-relevant abilities, faction allegiances in detail, the identity of the dead king, and the full timeline. Before character design begins, the project needs a proper lore bible pass built from the official press kit and the novels themselves, reviewed with the author.

## 4. Product Vision Statement

Shadow of Giants is the definitive interactive entry point into the Realm of Goryo. It is a fighting game where every match is a small human story inside a giant one: two champions of rival futures collide while gods reshape the horizon behind them. It is built natively for a phone held sideways, playable seriously with one thumb, honest about the player's time and money, and unmistakably itself: inked, brutal, mythic, and canon-faithful. In three years it should be the game kaiju fans name first when asked what to play on a phone, and the single largest driver of new readers to the novels.

## 5. What We Are Building (Recommended Concept)

This is the studio recommendation for the best game to make from this IP under the stated constraints (2D fighting, Android first, landscape, Mature rating, MK4 as art reference, MK Mobile as control reference). It will be pressure-tested and locked in the design brief.

**Core fights.** One-on-one duels between human-scale champions on a 2D gameplay plane, with teams of three and tag mechanics in the MK Mobile mold. Controls are tap for chained light attacks, swipe variants for heavies and launchers, hold to block, and two or three special-ability cards charged by a shared meter. Low execution demand, high decision demand: the skill is in timing, meter spend, tag choice, and reading the opponent.

**Kaiju as systems, never as skins.** Kaiju appear in three ways. As living stages: mid-fight arena events where Khulandra's tide surge floods the lowground or Goryo's passage shakes the arena and changes it. As raid bosses: an asynchronous Kaiju Siege mode where a team of three fights a screen-filling monster across phases, breaking parts and surviving scripted catastrophes; this is the flagship differentiator no MK Mobile clone has. As summons: the ultimate meter payoff for aligned champions is a single devastating kaiju intervention. Kaiju are not standard roster picks at launch; their scale is the point.

**Horrific Endings.** The finisher system adapts the gamebook directly. Instead of imitating Mortal Kombat's animated fatalities, a match-ending finisher smash-cuts to a full-screen inked comic splash panel with a caption: one of the "dozens of horrific endings," canon-flavored, brutal, collectible. This is cheaper to produce than animated gore, more distinctive, perfectly aligned with the comic-book story presentation, and it earns the M rating through horror and consequence rather than dismemberment sport.

**Comic-book story mode with choices.** Story is delivered in motion-comic panels adapting the saga in order, beginning with The Four Pillars. Between fights, occasional Make Your Escape choice nodes branch the path and can dead-end in a Horrific Ending panel, honoring the gamebook and giving chapters replay value. Season by season, the story mode walks the whole series.

**Launch roster shape.** Eight to ten champions across the factions, for illustration only until canon review: Noah Sato as an Ark-tech trickster, Kest as a transforming werefox rushdown, Satori as a mind-reading zoner who punishes predictable inputs, Tengi as a grim heavy whose theme is the culling, Arikura as a defensive stance fighter, a caudata vanguard from Anquisheng, a Mu dread-officer, and a Coalition ace. Goryo, Khulandra, and Raisha anchor stages and Sieges.

**Presentation and the MK4 reference.** MK4 and MK Mobile are both 3D games shot from the side. The production recommendation is the same trick: rigged 3D characters locked to a 2D gameplay plane, which reads as 2D, matches the reference feel, and makes the animation volume of a fighting game achievable with our asset pipeline, while cutscenes and finishers live in heavy-ink comic art. The alternative, fully hand-animated 2D sprites, is the single most expensive road in this genre and is not recommended for a small team. Final call happens in the design brief.

## 6. Design Philosophy (Pillars)

Every future decision, from a move's damage number to a store price, gets tested against these seven pillars. When two pillars conflict, the earlier one wins.

**Pillar 1: The novels are the engine.** Canon is the source of power. Characters fight the way their books say they are, story beats land in the order the saga tells them, and nothing ships that the rights holder has not blessed. Invented content (new champions, side stories) is proposed as canon-compatible and offered back to the author, so the game deepens the universe instead of forking it. The measure: a reader should feel the game was made by people who finished the books, and a player should finish a season wanting to buy one.

**Pillar 2: Kaiju are weather, not costumes.** The moment a kaiju is just another health bar the same size as a human, the IP's core awe is dead. Kaiju act on the game the way they act on the world of the novels: as environment, catastrophe, deity, and endgame. Anything that shrinks them, literally or tonally, is out, no matter how monetizable a playable pocket Khulandra would be.

**Pillar 3: One thumb, real decisions.** Built for touch from the first prototype: no virtual arcade stick, no motion inputs, no combo executable only on glass with claw grip. Depth comes from timing, spacing on one axis, meter economy, tag logic, and mind games. A commuter standing on a train must be able to play at full competitive depth. Target from day one: input-to-impact under 80 ms and a fight readable at arm's length on a 6-inch screen.

**Pillar 4: Readable brutality.** We are Mature the way the Realm of Goryo is mature: lethal, horrifying, consequential, never pornographic about cruelty. Violence must always communicate (whose hit, how hard, what changed) before it decorates. Horrific Endings deliver the M-rated punch as authored horror panels with dramatic weight. Hard lines regardless of rating: no sexual violence, no torture presented as reward, no real-world extremist iconography, nothing involving minors. If a piece of gore does not serve fear or story, it is noise, and it goes.

**Pillar 5: Sessions that respect a life.** A complete, satisfying session is one fight, two to four minutes. Progress is steady for twenty minutes a day; nothing meaningful requires a two-hour grind or a 4 a.m. login. Energy systems, if any exist at all, pace content, never hold fun hostage. Notifications invite, never guilt. The game should feel like a favorite gym, not a slot machine.

**Pillar 6: Fair by architecture.** Whatever the business model becomes (Section 10), three commitments are structural: every fighter is earnable through play, paid power can never exceed earnable power in any competitive mode, and all odds and prices are transparent. We monetize expression, convenience, and story, not victory. This is both ethics and strategy: fighting-game communities are small, loud, and permanent, and trust is the only durable moat a small studio has.

**Pillar 7: Mobile-native craft.** Android first is a craft statement, not a downgrade. Sixty frames per second on 2021 mid-range hardware, thermals that survive a thirty-minute session, an install under 300 MB at launch, instant resume, offline-friendly single player, landscape-sensor by default. Every art and tech choice is made for the phone in a hand, not ported down to it.

## 7. Product Goals

Goals are staged in three horizons. Numbers are initial targets to validate in soft launch, not promises.

### Horizon 1: Prove the fight (roughly months 0 to 6)

Goal 1: a vertical slice on real Android hardware: two champions (recommendation: Kest versus Tengi, maximum contrast), one living stage with a Khulandra event, full touch combat, one Horrific Ending each, and one motion-comic story sequence. Goal 2: hit the feel bar: 60 fps on a Snapdragon 695-class device, input-to-impact under 80 ms, and blind playtesters can explain the controls after one match with no tutorial. Goal 3: lock the art identity: one style test approved that unifies in-fight rendering and comic panels, reviewed against the IP with the rights holder. Goal 4: secure the foundation: rights and adaptation terms agreed in writing, and the lore bible v1 completed from the press kit and novels. Exit criterion: a stranger plays the slice for fifteen minutes, wants a rematch, and can name what makes it not-Mortal-Kombat.

### Horizon 2: Prove the game (roughly months 6 to 18)

Goal 5: Android soft launch in two to three test markets with eight to ten champions, Story Act 1 (The Four Pillars, three chapters with branching nodes), Kaiju Siege v1 (Khulandra), daily objectives, and one live event. Goal 6: retention and quality bars: day-1 retention at or above 35 percent, day-7 at or above 12 percent, crash-free sessions at or above 99.5 percent, store rating at or above 4.3. Goal 7: economy sanity: chosen business model validated with players spending on expression and story, zero pay-to-win complaints that survive audit. Goal 8: prove the transmedia loop with trackable game-to-book conversions (a measurable click-through from the season's end screen to the corresponding novel). Exit criterion: metrics say people return without being nagged, and the author is proud of Act 1.

### Horizon 3: Prove the platform (roughly months 18 to 36)

Goal 9: global Android launch, then iOS. Goal 10: live cadence of one themed season per book arc (The Culling season, Baptism season, and onward), each adding champions, a Siege boss, story chapters, and Horrific Endings. Goal 11: roster at twenty-plus champions with every faction represented and at least one fully game-original, author-approved champion folded back toward canon. Goal 12: asynchronous PvP leagues (defense teams, MK Mobile style) healthy at launch of the mode; a decision gate, with real data, on whether real-time PvP is worth its enormous netcode cost. Goal 13: the game is self-sustaining: revenue covers the live team and funds the next horizon.

## 8. Long-Term Vision (Years 3 to 5)

The end state is that Realm of Goryo becomes a two-medium universe with the game as its interactive front door. Concretely, five ambitions, in priority order. First, the complete saga playable: story mode eventually spans every published novel, and new novels launch day-and-date with their game season, turning each book release into a live event. Second, the kaiju grow into the endgame: Sieges evolve into cooperative guild-scale hunts (cells of players against Raisha across a week), and a Kaiju Duel mode, where players finally pilot the gods themselves in a deliberately different, weighty control scheme, ships only when it can be done without violating Pillar 2. Third, community as canon: seasonal community events whose outcomes the author can acknowledge, creator tools for sharing replay panels of Horrific Endings, and a small competitive circuit if and only if real-time PvP ships and holds. Fourth, platform breadth: iOS, then evaluate a PC port for the fighting-game community, keeping mobile as the design master. Fifth, franchise optionality: the engine, pipeline, and art identity built here become the studio's reusable core, so a second Goryo game (the Make Your Escape narrative adventure is the obvious candidate) costs a fraction of the first.

What long-term success looks like in one sentence: when someone finishes a Realm of Goryo novel, they install the game the same night, and when someone finishes a season, they buy the next book.

## 9. Audience, Rating, and Positioning

**Primary audience:** players 17 to 35 on Android who play or have lapsed from Mortal Kombat Mobile, Injustice Mobile, or Shadow Fight, and who want darker fiction and shorter sessions than console fighters offer. **Secondary:** the existing Realm of Goryo readership, and the broader kaiju fandom (Godzilla, Gamera, Pacific Rim, GigaBash audiences) that has no serious mobile fighter of its own. **Rating:** ESRB M (17+) and PEGI 18, earned through blood, intense violence, and horror themes per Pillar 4, cleared with the rights holder per Section 3.4, and compliant with Google Play content policy.

**Positioning against the field.** Against MK Mobile and Injustice Mobile: same effortless control language, but an original dark saga instead of a licensed greatest-hits roster, kaiju-scale raid drama they structurally cannot copy, and a fairness posture their economies gave up years ago. Against Shadow Fight Arena: comparable indie-scale craft, but with a literary universe, comic-book storytelling, and finishers with actual authorship. Against console fighters: not a competitor; we are the fighting game for the 94 percent of a life that happens away from a couch. The one-line position: the kaiju fighting saga in your pocket.

## 10. Business Model Philosophy

The model is an open decision for the design brief; the philosophy is not. Three lanes are on the table. Lane A, premium: a one-time price, no in-app purchases, optional paid story expansions per book arc; lowest trust risk, lowest revenue ceiling, weakest live-ops fit. Lane B, free with a season pass: free install, cosmetic and pass monetization, all champions earnable; the modern default, and the current recommendation. Lane C, free-to-play collector in the full MK Mobile mold: highest ceiling, highest risk to Pillar 6 and to a small team's soul; adopted only with strict guardrails. Whatever lane wins, the Pillar 6 commitments are non-negotiable, and the pricing of Horrific Endings and champion cosmetics must feel like buying comic covers, not like paying rent.

## 11. Platform and Technology Intent

Engine: Unity 6 LTS with URP, the mobile-safe default. Target: Android first, landscape orientation, ARM64, minimum API level around 26, IL2CPP for release builds, 60 fps on 2021 mid-range devices, install under 300 MB at launch. Character pipeline per Section 5: rigged 3D characters on a side-locked camera and a 2D gameplay plane, with generated and hand-finished assets; comic panels as 2D illustrated art. Development follows the local studio pipeline: the deliverable at every milestone is a complete Unity project the team opens and builds to an APK locally; nothing depends on a hosted service to play. Single-player and asynchronous modes are architected offline-first; any real-time PvP is treated as a separate, gated project because of its cost.

## 12. Risks and Open Questions

**Rights and partnership (highest priority).** The game cannot proceed past prototype without a written license or partnership with Dane G. Kroll covering the novels, characters, and marks, plus an approval workflow for depictions. If the project team is the rights holder, this becomes a simple internal sign-off, but it must be explicit either way. **Tone escalation.** The M rating exceeds the mainline novels' depicted intensity; the Horrific Endings framing is our answer, and it needs the author's yes. **Canon depth.** Marketing copy is not a lore bible; character abilities and appearances must come from the books and press kit before concept art hardens. **Animation volume.** Fighting games die on animation cost; the 3D-on-a-plane pipeline is the mitigation, and the vertical slice exists to prove it. **Scope.** A fighting game plus raids plus branching story is three games; horizons and the two-champion slice are the discipline. **Live-ops load.** A seasonal cadence assumes a small but real live team; Lane A exists as the fallback if that team never materializes. **PvP netcode.** Real-time fighting netcode (rollback) on mobile is a major engineering bet; it is explicitly deferred behind a data gate. **Discoverability.** An original dark IP has no license marquee; the kaiju hook, the Horrific Endings shareability, and the author's existing readership are the wedge, and store featuring should be pursued on the strength of the art identity.

**Open questions for the rights holder and the design brief:** the identity and canon abilities of each champion; which king is dead; visual canon for Goryo, Khulandra, and Raisha; the business model lane; whether any kaiju is ever playable and under what fiction; title approval; and how far story seasons may run ahead of, or must trail behind, the novels.

---

## 13. What This Game Is Not

It is not a kaiju-versus-kaiju brawler at its core; the gods stay god-sized (a Kaiju Duel mode may come later, on Pillar 2's terms). It is not a port or an imitation of console Mortal Kombat; it borrows the control grammar of MK Mobile and the grim confidence of MK4's era, nothing else. It is not a gacha casino; Pillar 6 is structural. It is not an open-world game, a base builder, or an idle game wearing a fighter's skin. It is not real-time PvP at launch. And it is not a fan work: it ships with the rights holder's blessing or it does not ship.
